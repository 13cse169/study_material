GENERATIVE ADVERSARIAL NETWORKS

Generator vs Discriminator — adversarial training
🔹GAN Framework

Two networks compete: Generator G creates fake data from noise, Discriminator D tries to tell real from fake. They train against each other in a minimax game:

min_G max_D V(D,G) = E[logD(x)] + E[log(1-D(G(z)))]
Python · DCGAN Generator
class Generator(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, z_dim=100, ngf=64):
        super().__init__()
        self.main = nn.Sequential(
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(z_dim, ngf*8, 4, 1, 0, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(ngf*8), nn.ReLU(True),
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(ngf*8, ngf*4, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(ngf*4), nn.ReLU(True),
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(ngf*4, 3, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.Tanh())  # → (B,3,16,16)

DIFFUSION MODELS

The technology behind Stable Diffusion and DALL-E
🔹How Diffusion Works

Add Gaussian noise to data over T steps (forward process), then train a U-Net to reverse this (denoise) step by step.

Forward: q(xₜ|xₜ₋₁) = N(√(1-βₜ)xₜ₋₁, βₜI) Reverse: pθ(xₜ₋₁|xₜ) = N(μθ(xₜ,t), σₜ²I) Loss: L = E[||ε - εθ(√ᾱₜx₀ + √(1-ᾱₜ)ε, t)||²]
🎨SDXL, Stable Diffusion, DALL-E 3, Imagen are all based on diffusion models conditioned on text embeddings (CLIP). The U-Net is replaced by a Transformer (DiT) in the latest models.

Q01What is the difference between a GAN and a VAE?
hard+
VAE (Variational Autoencoder): Encoder maps input to distribution (μ, σ) in latent space; reparameterisation trick allows backprop through sampling; Decoder reconstructs. Loss = reconstruction + KL divergence. Output is blurry (optimises pixel-wise MSE/BCE). Good latent space structure — can interpolate meaningfully. GAN: No explicit latent space encoding; generator maps random noise to samples; discriminator provides training signal. Loss is adversarial — hard to train (mode collapse, instability). Produces sharper, more realistic images. Modern choice: Diffusion models outperform both for image generation.
Q02What is knowledge distillation?
hard+
Knowledge distillation (Hinton 2015) trains a small student model to mimic a large teacher model. Instead of training on hard labels (one-hot), student is trained on teacher's soft probabilities (dark knowledge). Loss = α·CrossEntropy(y_hard, y_student) + (1-α)·KL(y_teacher_T, y_student_T) where T is temperature (>1 softens distributions, reveals inter-class relationships). Distilled models are 4-10x smaller with only 1-5% accuracy drop. Used to create: DistilBERT (40% smaller than BERT), MobileNet (from ResNet), TinyBERT. Also applied in DRL (policy distillation) and multimodal learning.
Q03Explain model quantisation techniques.
hard+
Quantisation reduces parameter precision from FP32 to INT8/INT4/binary. Types: Post-training quantisation (PTQ): Quantise after training with a calibration dataset. Simple, no retraining. Works well at INT8 (≈1% accuracy drop). Quantisation-aware training (QAT): Simulate quantisation during training using fake quantisation nodes. Better accuracy at lower bit-widths. NF4 (Normal Float 4): Used in QLoRA — 4-bit representation optimised for normally distributed weights. GGUF: Mixed precision format for CPU inference (used by llama.cpp). Speed gains: INT8 is 2x faster than FP32 on CPUs; INT4 enables 70B models on consumer GPUs.
